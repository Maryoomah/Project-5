Nigerian jollof rice, also known as "party jollof," is a vibrant and flavorful one-pot dish that's a staple in West African cuisine. It is believed to have originated in Senegal from the Wolof people, the name "Jollof" comes from their ethnic group. Variations exist across West Africa, including Ghana, Gambia, and Sierra Leone, each with unique spices and ingredients. It is a source of friendly competition and debate, with passionate discussions about the "best" jollof recipe. A symbol of celebration and community, often served at weddings, holidays, and other special occasions. What it is:

  • A dish made with long-grain rice simmered in a rich tomato and pepper stew.
  • Packed with spices like thyme, bay leaves, curry powder, and sometimes Scotch bonnet peppers for heat
  • Often cooked with meat (chicken, beef, or fish) and vegetables (onions, bell peppers).
  • Known for its complex flavors, combining sweetness, savoryness, and a kick of spice.
  • Typically served as a main course with fried plantains (dodo) and coleslaw on the side.
